Average is the new losing

Yazan Awwad · March 2026 · 5 min read

There was a time when being average was safe. You showed up, did the work, kept your head down, and the system rewarded you with stability. That time is over.

I don't say that to be harsh. I say it because I think a lot of people my age are operating on an outdated assumption — that if you just do what's expected of you, things will work out. Graduate, apply, interview, get hired, repeat. The problem is that everyone around you is doing exactly the same thing, in exactly the same way, with exactly the same credentials.

When everyone is average, average becomes invisible.

The world got more competitive while we weren't paying attention

Think about what a recruiter's inbox looks like today. Hundreds of applications for a single position. CVs that all follow the same template, cover letters that all open with "I am writing to express my interest in," portfolios that all look like they were built from the same theme.

It's not that people are unqualified. Most of them are perfectly qualified. That's exactly the problem. Qualified is the baseline now, not the differentiator. A degree doesn't make you stand out anymore — it just gets you in the door. What you do after you walk through it is the only thing that matters.

I realised this properly during my semester in Norway. I was surrounded by students from twenty different countries, all smart, all motivated, all working hard. The ones who stood out weren't necessarily the most technically skilled. They were the ones who had a perspective. A point of view. Something to say that nobody else in the room was saying.

That's what I mean by standing out. Not being louder. Not performing confidence you don't have. Having something real and specific about you that nobody else can copy and paste.

Average is a choice, even when it doesn't feel like one

Here's the uncomfortable part: most people don't choose average consciously. They drift into it. They take the safe option at every fork in the road, not because they're lazy, but because the safe option is always more immediately comfortable than the risky one.

Don't take the harder course — you might get a lower grade. Don't apply for the competitive program — you might get rejected. Don't publish your writing — people might disagree with it. Don't reach out to that person — they probably won't respond.

Every one of those small safe choices is completely rational in isolation. Stacked on top of each other over months and years, they produce a person who has carefully avoided every situation that might have made them interesting.

The risk of rejection feels very real. The cost of staying average is invisible — until it isn't.

Standing out is not about being exceptional at everything

I want to be clear about something, because I think "standing out" gets misread as pressure to be extraordinary across the board. It doesn't mean that. You don't need to be the best student, the most charismatic person in the room, and a published researcher simultaneously.

It means being genuinely, specifically yourself — and being willing to show that in public. A specific combination of interests, experiences, and perspectives that is yours and nobody else's. An MIS student who spent a semester in Norway, writes about technology and growth, and thinks seriously about how systems affect people — that's already a specific person. Not a category.

The goal isn't to manufacture uniqueness. It's to stop hiding the uniqueness you already have.

What I actually do about it

I try to apply for things I'm not sure I'll get. I write things I'm not sure people will agree with. I take on work that's slightly beyond what I'm comfortable doing, because that's the only way the boundary of "comfortable" ever moves.

I get rejected. I write things that don't land. I take on work and find it harder than I expected. None of that has killed me. All of it has made the next attempt slightly less frightening.

The world is competitive. That's not going to change. The question is whether you're going to treat that as a reason to play it safe, or as a reason to give yourself something real to compete with.

Being average in a competitive world isn't neutral. It's a slow way of losing ground to everyone who decided not to be.
